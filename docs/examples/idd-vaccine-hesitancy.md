# Intervention Design Document: Vaccine Hesitancy

> **Source artifact.** This is the raw IDD as provided for ACE pipeline stress-testing.
> See [`idd-stress-test-observations.md`](idd-stress-test-observations.md) for the analysis of this IDD's gaps, verification strategy, and what it reveals about ACE skill requirements.
> This IDD is the harder of the two example cases because the focus group phase does not map cleanly onto the existing Connect delivery model (atomic photo + GPS + form).

## Overview

To identify the key drivers of under-vaccination in Sokoto State—including hesitancy (beliefs, perceived risks and benefits, social influence) and access and service-delivery barriers—and to test whether structured household follow-up visits by FLWs can improve vaccination uptake among zero- and low-dose children.

This work has two stages:

- **Stage 1:** Focus group discussions with community members to understand barriers and enablers.
- **Stage 2:** A household follow-up pilot where FLWs revisit under-vaccinated households to understand and try to overcome barriers.

## Background

A preliminary literature review identified the following key barriers to childhood vaccination in Sokoto:

**System-level barriers:**

- Vaccine shortages and inconsistent supply
- Long distances to health centers and weak cold chain logistics
- Shortage of health workers and poor provider attitudes
- Conflict and displacement disrupting services

**Socio-cultural and individual barriers:**

- Misinformation and mistrust: beliefs that vaccines cause infertility, violate religious tenets, or cause harm
- Low prioritization: children only vaccinated during outbreak campaigns rather than on routine schedule
- Opportunity cost of time, lost wages, and transportation
- Previous negative experiences
- Gender dynamics: limited female decision-making power, male household head refusal, influence of elders
- Preference for traditional remedies or campaign-based over facility-based immunization
- Lower health literacy, particularly among Hausa/Fulani communities and younger mothers

Known enabling factors include: framing vaccination as making one a good parent; trust built through local leaders and media; referral cards from trusted traditional barbers.

## Stage 1: Focus Group Discussions

### Who attends the Focus Groups

We are interested in Focus Groups generally consisting of people with undervaccinated children. We have thought of the following groups of people:

- Women, close to PHC (<30 min), under-vaccinated children
- Women, remote (1+ hr from PHC), under-vaccinated children
- Women, close to PHC, vaccinated children (comparison)
- Women, remote, vaccinated children (comparison)
- Men, close to PHC, mixed vaccination status
- Men, remote, mixed vaccination status

We are not certain how hard it is to identify and recruit people in these groups. We may need to discuss with LLOs what is realistic.

### Focus Group Questions

**Service delivery**

- Is it common for parents in your community to vaccinate their babies?
- When parents vaccinate, is it mostly at health facilities or other locations? What types?
- What has your experience at vaccination sessions at health facilities been like? What did you like and not like?
- If you have attended a vaccination session outside of a facility, what was that experience like?

**Barriers to vaccination**

- Are there times when you wanted to vaccinate your child but faced obstacles? What were they?
- How far do you live from the nearest health facility? How does that affect your decision to vaccinate?
- Are there times of year or circumstances when it is harder to get your child vaccinated?

**Personal views on vaccination**

- What do you see as the value of vaccination?
- What do you see as the risks?
- Are there specific vaccines you are more hesitant about than others?
- Have you ever delayed or skipped a vaccination? What led to that decision?
- For those with multiple children: how has your approach to vaccination changed across your children? What influenced those changes?
- Who in your family or community do you talk to when deciding whether to vaccinate? Whose opinion matters most?

**Community views on vaccination**

- What have you heard other parents say about why they choose not to vaccinate or to delay? How common are those views?
- How has your community's approach to vaccination changed over the years?
- Are there specific vaccines parents in your community are more hesitant about? Why?
- Are there opinions about vaccination in your community that you personally disagree with?

**Program exposure (New Incentives / All Babies Are Equal)**

- Have you heard about the All Babies Are Equal program for vaccination?
- Have you participated? What do you think of it?

**Additional questions for men's groups**

- How involved are you in decisions about vaccinating your children?
- What would make you more or less likely to support your child being vaccinated?
- Are there concerns from religious leaders or elders in your community about vaccination? What do you think of those views?

### Outputs from Stage 1

- Summary of key themes from each group, prepared by LLO facilitators
- AI-assisted synthesis across groups identifying top barriers and potential intervention points
- Revised Stage 2 design recommendations based on findings

## Stage 2: Household Follow-Up Pilot

FLWs will conduct structured follow-up visits to households where children are identified as zero- or low-dose during door-to-door surveys. The core hypothesis is that personalized follow-up and relationship-building will meaningfully increase vaccination uptake—particularly if the FLW commits to returning, which creates accountability and increases the likelihood that caregivers follow through.

### How It Works

- We will identify households with low vaccination rates through CHC or other door to door campaigns.
- FLWs revisit these households on a roughly two-week cadence
- At each visit, FLWs conduct a barrier survey, take photos of vaccine cards (or document absence), and try tailored messaging based on the household's specific barriers
- FLWs may also call clients between visits for reminders or to answer questions

### Barrier-Specific Strategies to Test

- General encouragement and coaching and follow-up by FLWs
- Misinformation: involve trusted religious leaders or community healers
- Gender barriers: involve male household head directly; consider using male FLWs for men's conversations
- Scheduling: identify and communicate upcoming mobile clinic visits near the household
- Framing: test messaging that reinforces vaccination as something good parents do

### Training

FLWs should receive special training on motivational interviewing and barrier-specific messaging. Ideally, these should be built into the Connect Learn app though LLOs may have additional training and guidance to provide.

### Success Metrics

- **Primary:** rate of vaccination among enrolled zero- and low-dose households by end of pilot
- **Secondary:** visits completed per household; barrier types identified; messaging strategies used
- **Learning:** which barriers are most prevalent; which messaging strategies are most associated with positive outcomes
